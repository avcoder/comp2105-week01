Animation in Web Design

Why Animate?

Have you ever had a day at work where you were constantly put towards a new task as you were ramping up on the current one? It feels jarring at best, and completely frustrating and time-wasting at worst. In recent years, employers at big companies have begun to consider the cost of context-shifting—the time spent re-adjusting your brain to a different task adds up, causes frustration in employees, and thus: loses money. It follows that User Experience on a website works the same way.

Sarah Drasner.
The Importance of context-shifting in UX patterns

Does the animation provide any useful information about the interaction?

Does it feel like it’s responding to you as the user?

What sort of emotional response does it trigger for you?

Animation that’s worth adding should make things better on some level—think progressive enrichment, or making the interaction richer for browsers that support it. If it’s not adding something—either in terms of use or style—then leave it out.

Val Head.
UI Animation and UX: A Not-So-Secret Friendship

...Animation allows the user to continue thinking about the task domain, with no need to shift contexts to the interface domain. By eliminating sudden visual changes, animation lessens the chance that the user is surprised.

Scott E. Hudson and John T. Stasko (1993)

12 Principles of Animation

Watch this brilliant vignette illustrating the 12 basic principles of animation as developed by the folks at Walt Disney Studios, including Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston, during the 1930s. Each of these principles, to some degree are employed in motion graphic user interface components of modern web applications. Let this short video begin the creative inspiration. Directed and animated by Vincenzo Lodigiani, taken from "The Illusion of Life" by Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston.

1. ___________________?


Illusion of weight / volume as character moves

Squash & Stretch

Illusion of weight / volume as character moves

Also useful in animating dialogue and doing facial expressions, from a bouncing ball to the body weight of a person walking. This is the most important element you will be required to master and will be used often.

2. ___________________?


Prepares a major action a character is about to perform

Anticipation

Prepares a major action a character is about to perform

For example, starting to run, jump or change expression. A dancer does not just leap off the floor. A backwards motion occurs before the forward action is executed. The backward motion is the anticipation. Similarly, a pitcher's wind-up or a golfers' back swing.

3. ___________________?


The presentation of an idea so that it is clear.

Staging

The presentation of an idea so that it is clear.

A pose or action should clearly communicate to the audience the attitude, mood, reaction or idea of the character. In addition, the effective use of long, medium, or close up shots, as well as camera angles also helps in telling the story. Staging directs the audience's attention to the story or idea being told. Background and animation should work together as a pictorial unit in a scene.

Staging

The presentation of an idea so that it is clear.

A pose or action should clearly communicate to the audience the attitude, mood, reaction or idea of the character. In addition, the effective use of long, medium, or close up shots, as well as camera angles also helps in telling the story. Staging directs the audience's attention to the story or idea being told. Background and animation should work together as a pictorial unit in a scene.

4. ___________________?


Starts at the first drawing and works drawing to drawing to the end of the scene / Charted with key drawings at intervals throughout the scene.


Straight Ahead &
Pose to Pose

Starts at the first drawing and works drawing to drawing to the end of the scene / Charted with key drawings at intervals throughout the scene.

Straight ahead animation starts at the first drawing and works drawing to drawing to the end of a scene. You can lose size, volume, and proportions with this method, but it does have spontaneity and freshness. Fast, wild action scenes are done this way.

Pose to Pose is more planned out and charted with key drawings done at intervals throughout the scene. Size, volumes, and proportions are controlled better this way, as is the action.

5. ___________________?


When the main body of a character stops, all other parts continue to catch up to the main mass. Nothing stops all at once.

Follow through and overlapping

When the main body of a character stops, all other parts continue to catch up to the main mass. Nothing stops all at once.

For example: arms, long hair, clothing, coat tails or a dress, floppy ears or a long tail (these follow the path of action). This is follow through. Overlapping action is when the character changes direction while his clothes or hair continues forward. For instance, when Goofy starts to run, but his head, ears, upper body, and clothes do not keep up with his legs.

6. ___________________?


As the action starts, we have more drawings near the starting pose, one or two in the middle, and more drawings near the next pose

Ease in and Ease out

As the action starts, we have more drawings near the starting pose, one or two in the middle, and more drawings near the next pose

Slow-ins and slow-outs soften the action, making it more life-like.

7. ___________________?


Actions follow a slightly circular path. This gives animation a more natural flow

Arcs

Actions follow a slightly circular path. This gives animation a more natural flow

This is especially true of the human figure and the action of animals. Arcs give animation a more natural action and better flow. Think of natural movements in the terms of a pendulum swinging. All arm movement, head turns and even eye movements are executed on an arcs.

8. ___________________?


An additional action used as a supplement of the main action in order to reinforce it and add more dimension

Secondary Action

An additional action used as a supplement of the main action in order to reinforce it and add more dimension

For example: A character is angrily walking toward another character. The walk is forceful, aggressive, and forward leaning. The leg action is just short of a stomping walk. The secondary action is a few strong gestures of the arms working with the walk. Think of the walk as the primary action and arm swings, head bounce and all other actions of the body as secondary or supporting action.

9. ___________________?


More drawings between poses slow and smooth the action. Fewer drawings make the action faster and crisper

Timing

More drawings between poses slow and smooth the action. Fewer drawings make the action faster and crisper

A variety of slow and fast timing within a scene adds texture and interest to the movement. Also, there is timing in the acting of a character to establish mood, emotion, and reaction to another character or situation. Studying movement of performers on stage and in frame by frame film examination will aid you in understanding timing for animation.

10. _________________?


Remaining true to reality–just presenting it in a more extreme form

Exaggeration

Remaining true to reality–just presenting it in a more extreme form

Exaggeration is not extreme distortion of a drawing or extremely broad, violent action all the time. It's like a caricature of facial features, expressions, poses, attitudes and actions. Exaggeration in a walk or an eye movement or even a head turn will give your film more appeal. Use good taste and common sense to keep from becoming too theatrical and excessively animated.

11. _________________?


Taking into account forms in three-dimensional space giving them volume and weight

Solid Drawings

Taking into account forms in three-dimensional space giving them volume and weight

The basic principles of drawing form, weight, volume solidity and the illusion of three dimension apply to animation as it does to academic drawing. Three dimensional is movement in space. The fourth dimension is movement in time

12. _________________?


A quality of charm–able to please the viewer's eyes. It is the charisma of the action.

Appeal

A quality of charm–able to please the viewer's eyes. It is the charisma of the action.

A live performer has charisma. An animated character has appeal. Appealing animation does not mean just being cute and cuddly. All characters have to have appeal whether they are heroic, villainous, comic or cute. Appeal, includes an easy to read design, clear drawing, and personality development that will capture and involve the audience's interest. It 'appeals' to the mind as well as to the eye

Not all of the 12 principles apply equally to work on the web or to modern tools. Some matter more than others for animating interfaces. A solid understanding of Timing, Follow-through, Appeal, Anticipation, and Squash and Stretch will be useful in web design, for example. While concepts like Staging and Solid Drawing, however, are mostly irrelevant.

Val Head.

One primary way to use animation is in: Stateful Transitions which can guide users between task flows such as from page to page, or field to field.

They're useful for indicating: 1) Change in task flow location 2) Where you've been 3) Where you are now

Goto: http://fluevog.com and search for 'shoe'.

Notice when you're searching, suddenly the whole page changes with a blue backdrop to let user know you're in a new context, that you don't need to see previous page since you just want search results.

Stateful transitions and supplemental animations

A secondary way is to use: Supplemental Animation which accentuates secondary information to the main state. For example, alerts, notifications. updates.

They're useful for indicating: 1) Changes in information 2) What is possible for users 3) Important details that shouldn't be overlooked

Goto: https://www.good.is/features and hover over each article row.

Notice a subtitle comes in and gives more supplemental info. Also, if you continue scrolling down to the bottom, they're assuming if you've gotten this far, you probably liked this article so rather than putting share links, they put a share bar. It's a nice-to-have for users that they might find useful.

Rachel Nabors.
Stateful transitions and supplemental animations

A third way is to use: Causal Effects which shows when something has caused something else to happen. For example, loaders/spinners indicate when the server has gotten enough info to load the page properly. Hover effects indicate that you are the cause of this change.

Goto: https://codepen.io/rachelnabors/pen/xGNYjV and hover over the menu causing it to move in anticipation of where it might go. Helps with cognitive prep / context switching.

Causal effects and decorative animations
